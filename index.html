I Accept the
Magic Anyway

Abstract

In an abandoned monastery in Spain, dripping water became footsteps. This paper asks whether Predictive Processing can serve as compositional dramaturgy: a four-state arc from sounds with clear sources through rising ambiguity to aberrant salience — the condition in which the brain's filtering mechanism fails and everything demands attention equally (Kapur, 2003). The paper then asks what keeps that state from tipping into dissociation. The answer found in practice is what Ihde (2007) calls the expressive dimension of voice: the layer that persists in a narrator's voice when its semantic content withdraws. The paper documents the compositional decisions behind I Accept the Magic Anyway, a collaborative headphone & subwoofer listening installation made with Mischa Nijhof for the exhibition Resounding Territories in Lisbon.

Research Question

Can Predictive Processing serve as the theoretical basis for a compositional language and dramaturgy?

Sub-question: What makes aberrant salience navigable rather than merely disorienting?

Introduction

Personal Position

One stormy night in Spain, I slept in an abandoned monastery. Water dripped into a puddle next to my room. Within minutes, the dripping had turned into footsteps. I checked several times whether anyone was there. Nothing in the acoustic signal had changed. What had changed was how my nervous system was reading it. Under darkness, isolation, and exhaustion, a sound I could easily identify had become loaded with ambiguous meaning. Aberrant salience is not the absence of meaning but its excess: not just source-uncertainty, but a felt urgency that nothing can be dismissed.

Two earlier observations sharpened this. At a concert in Lisbon, experimental ambient music engaged or relaxed one listener and sent another into anxious introspection. These experiences pointed to the same gap: aberrant salience works as a perceptual mechanism, but without an anchor, it tips toward one extreme or the other. The narrator in this piece is that anchor.

In my bachelor's work, I tried to make my grieving process audible. This piece here turned out to focus on transitional spaces. This was not conceived as a continuation of that. Looking back, it is one.

Research Context & Landmark References

Predictive Processing is a well-established theory of perception: the brain continuously compares incoming sensory data against stored predictions, while mismatches drive attention toward what is surprising or significant (Clark, 2013; Seth, 2021). Sá's taxonomy of continuity and discontinuity maps closely onto PP: ambivalent discontinuity corresponds to rising prediction error, and radical discontinuity approximates aberrant salience (Sá, 2013). Schaeffer's listening taxonomy offers a structural parallel: écoute causale at one end, écoute réduite at the other. The movement from State 1 to State 3 in this piece follows roughly that sequence. Ihde's concept of the expressive dimension of voice (Ihde, 2007) provides the theoretical grounding for the sub-question.

The methodology is practice-based: findings come from compositional decisions, the documented failure of an earlier approach in State 3, and audience responses from Jardim do Bombarda. The paper proceeds through a literature review covering PP and aberrant salience, Schaeffer's listening modes and their limits, Ihde's dual dimension of voice, and the concept of Forced Grace; followed by a case study documenting the four-state structure of the piece.

The Four-State Arc

The compositional architecture maps directly onto the PP logic.

State 1
Low prediction error. Figurative, source-identifiable sounds: field recordings the brain can categorise without effort. The narrator names what is heard — not to add information but to give the listener permission to stop searching for a threat. Grammar modelled on Calvino's Palomar (1983): short declarative sentences, one observation at a time.
State 2
Prediction error rising. Sounds begin to morph; their sources become less certain. The narrator's grammar shifts in parallel: complete sentences give way to half-sentences, certainty becomes shared uncertainty. "Those were rain drops just now. Or steps. We don't know." The words 'or' and 'we' carry most of the weight.
State 3
Aberrant salience. Categorisation fails. The narrator says "I will speak less now" and then does, but does not disappear. What remains is Ihde's expressive dimension: breath, hesitation, fragments from a recorded self-interview with all semantic context removed. Around this, ebb and flow of composed grains replaces static chaos — density breathes, rises and recedes.
State 4
Forced Grace. The Litany Against Fear (Herbert, 1965) and the recognition "I've been here before" mark the transition out of aberrant salience. The narrator returns with one final broken sentence: "I'm feeling fear, but my mind knows it's not real, and that's wh…" The break is already the metacognitive act. Distorted violin and synth pads in major chords — something melancholic and aggressive alongside something warm.

Project Incubation · Literature Review

Literature
Review

Annotated bibliography — theoretical and practical references.

Research map: references, sub-questions, hypothesis, methods.

3.1 Predictive Processing & Aberrant Salience
Clark, A. (2013). Whatever next? Predictive brains, situated agents, and the future of cognitive science. Behavioral and Brain Sciences, 36(3), 181–204.

Clark's paper is a landmark synthesis of Predictive Processing theory. The brain continuously generates predictions about incoming sensory data and updates them based on prediction errors — mismatches between expectation and input. Crucially, Clark argues that affective and prosodic features of voice constitute a prediction layer separate from semantic content. This separation is the mechanism that allows the expressive dimension to remain as an anchor when semantic content withdraws in State 3.

Predictive Processing Prediction Error Active Inference
Seth, A. (2021). Being You: A New Science of Consciousness. Dutton.

Seth develops PP into a theory of consciousness as "controlled hallucination": the brain's best prediction of the causes of sensory signals, not a direct readout of reality. This framing reframes the monastery experience — the footsteps were not a failure of perception but its normal operation. Relevant for framing the compositional arc as designing controlled versions of a process that already happens.

Controlled Hallucination Consciousness Perception as Inference
Kapur, S. (2003). Psychosis as a state of aberrant salience. American Journal of Psychiatry, 160(1), 13–23.

Kapur defines aberrant salience as the breakdown of the brain's filtering mechanism: everything demands attention, nothing can be dismissed as unimportant. Developed in psychosis research, the mechanism is borrowed here as a compositional state — not a clinical one. It is not meaning gain but meaning overwhelm; the boundary between figuration and abstraction dissolves. The piece aims to occupy the zone between over-figuration (monastery footsteps) and dissociation (categorisation shutdown).

Aberrant Salience Salience Filter Meaning Overwhelm
Vuust, P. & Friston, K. (2022). Why music moves us. Neuroscience and Biobehavioral Reviews.

Applies the PP framework to music, arguing that emotional and cognitive responses to sound are triggered when "expectations are met or artfully violated" (Kringelbach et al., 2024, as cited in Ibanez, 2026). This is the clearest statement that composers design PP conditions — that the craft of composition is, in part, the craft of managing prediction error. Directly supports the claim that the four-state arc is a PP dramaturgy.

Musical Expectations Artful Violation
3.2 Schaeffer's Listening Modes & Their Limits
Schaeffer, P. (1966). Traité des objets musicaux. Éditions du Seuil.

Schaeffer's listening taxonomy is the structural parallel to the PP arc. Écoute causale traces sound to its source; écoute réduite listens to sound as pure material, stripped of source and context. The movement from State 1 to State 3 in this piece follows roughly that sequence. The key difference is method: Schaeffer's reduced listening required a known sound, a turntable loop, and explicit instruction. The monastery shift arrived without any technique. The piece uses Schaeffer's taxonomy as a map of states, not a method for getting there.

Écoute causale Écoute réduite Objet sonore Musique concrète
Bonnet, F.J. (2016). The Order of Sounds: A Sonorous Archipelago. Urbanomic/MIT Press.

Bonnet argues that reduced listening — hearing sound stripped of source and context — is impossible regardless of technique: listeners cannot stop bringing meaning to what they hear. This critique stands in State 3. The piece does not resolve this. It accepts it. The title came from exactly that: you accept what the experience gives you, regardless of whether it is true.

Limits of Reduced Listening Meaning-making
3.3 The Dual Dimension of Voice (Ihde)
Ihde, D. (2007). Listening and Voice: Phenomenologies of Sound (2nd ed.). State University of New York Press.

Ihde argues that voice carries two simultaneous layers: the semantic content of what is said, and the expressive dimension of how it is said — tone, breath, hesitation, rhythm. A sentence spoken with hesitation communicates something different from the same sentence spoken confidently, even when the words are identical. In PP terms, these are separate prediction layers. In this piece, it is the expressive dimension that makes aberrant salience navigable rather than merely overwhelming: when semantic content withdraws in State 3, warmth and rhythm remain as a stable prior.

Expressive Dimension Semantic Layer Voice Phenomenology
Westerkamp, H. (1989). Kits Beach Soundwalk. Transformations, Empreintes Digitales.

A direct practical reference for the narrator's function: a warm voice naming field recordings that navigates without instructing and moves toward dream-like abstraction. Westerkamp demonstrates that the expressive dimension can carry the piece even as semantic content becomes less central. The narrator in I Accept the Magic Anyway models this approach in State 1 and 2, then tests what happens when semantic content withdraws further in State 3.

Soundscape Composition Voice as Guide Field Recording
Jelinek, J. (2018). Zwischen. Faitiche.

A practical reference for what the expressive dimension can do in electroacoustic form without a narrator. Jelinek's album demonstrates that warmth, hesitation, and breath can be compositionally approximated through instrumental and electronic means alone. Relevant as a contrast case: this piece argues that the voice's expressive dimension is not easily replaceable by other compositional tools when it comes to navigability under aberrant salience.

Electroacoustic Composition Expressive Timbre
3.4 Forced Grace
Novack, D. (2022). Sound Design for Film: Liberating from Image, Seducing Surrender. PhD dissertation, Universidade Lusofona.

Novack argues that sound design works directly on the body, seducing the listener into releasing cognitive control — the body lets go first, the mind follows. Forced Grace is a useful contrast: it runs the other way. The body is afraid; the mind knows there is nothing to fear. Neither cancels the other. Grace and surrender are not synonyms, but share a structure: in both, the self stops managing. Heidegger's concept of Angst is a related reference: the mood in which the world's familiar structure breaks down, distinct from ordinary fear (Heidegger). Unlike Angst, Forced Grace does not dissolve observation.

Surrender Seduction Bodily Response Angst (Heidegger)
Heidegger, M. (1962). Being and Time (J. Macquarrie & E. Robinson, Trans.). Harper & Row. (Original work published 1927)

Heidegger distinguishes Angst from ordinary fear: where fear has a determinate object, Angst is the mood in which the world's familiar structure withdraws and nothing offers stable meaning. Unlike Angst, Forced Grace does not dissolve observation — the narrator's metacognitive act ("I know it is not real") holds both states simultaneously. Heidegger's distinction matters here because it clarifies what the piece does not do: it does not aim for the undifferentiated breakdown of Angst, but for the tension of holding fear and knowledge in the same moment.

Angst Mood Worldhood
Goldner, F.H. (1982). Pronoia. Social Problems, 30(1), 82–91.

Goldner names pronoia: the irrational belief that the world conspires to do you good — the positive counterpart to paranoia. He calls it irrational; the belief has no basis in fact. The piece tests a different version: pronoia reached through metacognition. The "anyway" in the title is not delusion. It is a decision. Mischa Nijhof's piece in the installation arrives at the same mechanism through warmth rather than the metacognitive fight: whether pronoia and paranoia together constitute a compositional axis is a question the paper opens without closing.

Pronoia Metacognition Paranoia
Sá, A. (2013). Continuity/discontinuity taxonomy for audiovisual instrument design. [Conference paper]

Sá maps audiovisual relationships along a continuity axis. Ambivalent discontinuity corresponds to rising prediction error (State 2); radical discontinuity approximates aberrant salience (State 3). The taxonomy was designed for audiovisual performance; this piece applies the sonic axis alone. The approximation is rough — radical discontinuity describes a functioning system registering a rupture, while aberrant salience describes one whose filtering has failed. The distinction matters compositionally.

Continuity/Discontinuity Ambivalent Discontinuity Radical Discontinuity
Calvino, I. (1983). Palomar. Einaudi.

Calvino's prose in Palomar provided the grammatical model for the State 1 narrator voice: short declarative sentences, one observation at a time. "Those are rain drops. The roof. The wind." The grammar was designed to give the listener permission to stop searching for a threat. Adriana Sá's observation after listening — that the naming had introduced doubt rather than resolved it — suggests naming a sound raises the possibility it might not have been there. A voice that identifies a sound is also a voice that could be wrong.

Narrator Grammar Observation
Herbert, F. (1965). Dune. Chilton Books.

The Litany Against Fear from Dune is used in State 4 of the piece. Not because it resolves anything, but because recitation is a metacognitive act: naming the fear while knowing its nature. "I must not fear. Fear is the mind-killer…" The structure of the litany — threat → void → self → recognition — matches the transition out of aberrant salience. The narrator's final broken sentence in State 4 breaks where the explanation would begin; that break is already the metacognitive act.

Findings & Open Questions

The Predictive Processing framework did ground a workable compositional dramaturgy. The four states map onto the logic of prediction error: low, rising, collapsed into aberrant salience, and then metacognitively held. The grammar of the narrator at each state was derived directly from this logic: at low prediction error, short declaratives that close uncertainty; at rising error, half-sentences and 'or' and 'we'; at collapsed categorisation, breath and fragments.

After the first intensity peak of State 3, the soundscape drops suddenly: light rain on a porch instead of the monastery chaos. The narrator steps back from the shared 'we': "Am I dissociating?" The question is not directed at the listener. The listener may find it anyway.

The sub-question found a practical answer, though not a clean one. The expressive dimension of voice, without semantic content, makes aberrant salience navigable in a way that pure abstraction does not. But the first version also held grain synthesis at constant maximum intensity. The current version breathes: density rises and recedes rather than flooding. Both changes happened together. Whether the expressive dimension alone would suffice, or whether the ebb and flow of the grain is doing equal work, remains an open question this piece cannot answer.

Several visitors reported State 4 as the narrator's story rather than their own. Whether that is a failure of the compositional design, a limitation of first-person voice, or an expected response to an autobiographical piece remains unresolved. After listening, Adriana Sá said she had already begun questioning her perception in the first minutes: "Did I actually hear the roof creak?" The naming that was designed to stabilise had instead introduced doubt. A voice that identifies a sound raises the possibility it might not have been there.

Further Research

The most immediate open question comes from the collaboration with Mischa Nijhof. Her piece was not built from PP theory — it began when she heard the monastery story and recognised her own experience in it. But the same mechanism is legible in both: aberrant salience that produces warmth rather than threat. Whether Pronoia and Paranoia together constitute a compositional axis others can navigate, and what positions exist between the poles, is a question this paper opens without closing.

The expressive dimension as a compositional tool also needs further development. This paper identifies it in one form: the narrator's voice. Whether it can function as an anchor in other forms — a recurring melodic fragment, a spatial signature, a timbral quality — and under what conditions, is a question this piece cannot answer alone.

What this paper points toward most directly is the relationship between scientific framework and perceptual experience: not art inspired by neuroscience, but art that makes a perceptual mechanism legible during the experience itself. The narrator's final sentence breaks where the explanation would begin. That was a compositional decision. Whether completing it would have made the PP mechanism audible without breaking the experience is a question a next piece would need to find out.

Process

Where It Came From

How the piece found its form: ideas pursued, discarded, and transformed.

1
Starting Point · Electronic Music & Groove Manipulation

The first question was about groove and micro-timing: how small rhythmic deviations create or destroy the feeling of flow. I started running experiments with 808-pattern manipulation, Roland TR step sequencers and Max 4 Live, deliberately pushing timing until the groove collapsed into something uncomfortable. At what point does something stop feeling musical and start feeling wrong? That boundary became the research question.

A concert in Lisbon pushed it further. The same experimental ambient music left one person in the room relaxed and sent another into anxious introspection. Same piece, same room, opposite landings. Aberrant salience works as a perceptual mechanism, but without an anchor it tips toward one extreme or the other. What acts as the anchor was the question.

2
Three-State Model · Predictive Processing & Benjamin Bardou

Before the monastery, the research question had a theoretical shape already: can sound control the degree of perceptual anchoring in audiovisual experience? I drew on Predictive Processing (PP) theory and the concept of aberrant salience from psychosis phenomenology to build a three-state compositional model. Over-anchored: predictions dominate, attention disengages. Navigable ambiguity: predictions waver, attention sharpens. Anchor-removed: predictions fail, hyper-significance emerges. The three states map onto Baudelaire's Hell, Spleen, and Ideal, a philosophical frame for what PP describes neurologically. The Ideal names the same cognitive mechanism as aberrant salience, but recasts it as awe rather than threat.

Then I came across Benjamin Bardou's AI-generated latent space morph videos, and the model became concrete. His project The Flow uses continuous morph transitions through recognisable scenes, visual material that challenges the predictive system hardest where two scenes merge. His three Baudelairean regimes map onto the PP model exactly. The question got specific: can sound shape whether a viewer's experience of those transitions lands as Hell, Spleen, or Ideal? During the transitions, the image anchors nothing. Sound has to.

3
The Monastery · Concept Becomes Experience

One stormy night in an abandoned monastery in Spain, dripping water became footsteps. The shift was involuntary. The nervous system performed it under darkness, exhaustion, isolation. This wasn't a designed experience; it was the research question arriving as a concrete event. What had been abstract (at what point does perception misfire?) became something felt from the inside: this is what it feels like. The theoretical draft was set aside. The monastery became the centre.

4
First State 3 · What Didn't Work

The first version of State 3 was grain synthesis at constant maximum density, a permanent wall of sound meant to make categorisation impossible. In practice, people switched off. Not heightened attention: shutdown. Maximum intensity has no texture. There is nothing to follow.

First State 3 (draft) — not used in final piece

The revision introduced ebb and flow: density rises and recedes rather than flooding. The nervous system can track something. That made it possible to stay in the state instead of fleeing it.

5
Imitation Gestures & the Problem of Instruction

An early approach to the narrator's role involved explicit instruction: asking the listener to imitate what they heard, or name the physical sensation of listening. It didn't work. Instruction pulls attention toward compliance. The listener starts managing their response instead of having one. The narrator's grammar was revised: no imperatives, no instructions. Observation only. Short declaratives that close uncertainty without directing behaviour.

Where It Could Go

Open questions and possible directions the research opens.

Bardou Collaboration

Benjamin Bardou's project The Flow works with the same question on the visual level: how AI-generated latent space morph transitions produce competing predictions and perceptual ambiguity. His three Baudelairean regimes map onto the PP three-state model exactly. A collaboration, with sound composed specifically for his visual material, would test whether the compositional logic here can steer the experience of those transitions: whether anchoring, ambiguity, and awe can be shaped by sound alone when the image does not resolve.

The Pronoia / Paranoia Axis

The collaboration with Mischa Nijhof began when she heard the monastery story and recognised her own experience in it, but the valence was opposite: warmth, not threat. Same mechanism, different landing. Goldner (1982) coined the term pronoia for this pole, the positive counterpart to paranoia, the sense that others regard you well. His frame is sociological: pronoia is produced by organisations that withhold negative feedback. But the perceptual distortion is real. Pronoids systematically misread how others actually regard them. This piece reaches the same place via metacognition. The anyway in the title is not delusion. It is a decision. Mischa's piece arrives there without that detour. Whether pronoia and paranoia constitute a compositional axis others can navigate, and what positions exist between the poles, is a question this research opens without closing.

RAVE · ML Hallucination as Compositional Material

The IRCAM RAVE plugin (Realtime Audio Variational autoEncoder) trains on field recordings and generates new sonic material from them. The idea is to feed it the monastery recordings and use the output for State 3, replacing the looped Schaeffer-style field recordings with something the network has learned and then distorted. The less data it trains on, the more it hallucinates. The output becomes unstable: sounds that are almost, but not quite, the source. That instability is structurally the same as what Bardou's latent space morphs do visually, material caught between recognition and failure. Whether RAVE audio could work as a sonic parallel, and whether it would sharpen or destabilise the navigability of State 3, is open.

Art & Science

This paper is practice-based and does not run a user study. The audience response data gathered at Jardim do Bombarda was informal. A next iteration could design a proper perceptual study: controlled conditions, defined hypotheses, quantitative and qualitative measures. The questions are testable, particularly whether the expressive dimension alone accounts for navigability, or whether the ebb and flow of the grain synthesis is doing equal work.

The goal from the beginning was not to make a piece about neuroscience. I wanted to make a piece that is neuroscience, the kind of installation you leave having understood something, not just felt it. Not a choreography inspired by neuron movement, but something that enacts a perceptual mechanism. What the piece shows, if it works, is that fear and grace are the same state. The difference is the frame.
